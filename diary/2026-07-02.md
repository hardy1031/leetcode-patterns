## Theme: Floyd's Cycle Detection — Memorize vs Understand

Solved 287 (Find the Duplicate Number) using both hash set and Floyd's algorithm. Floyd's is counter-intuitive: treat the array as an implicit linked list where index i points to nums[i]. The duplicate creates a cycle, and finding the cycle's entrance equals finding the duplicate — the same structure as 142 (Linked List Cycle II).

In an interview, the right strategy is: lead with the hash set solution (O(n) space, easy to explain), then offer Floyd's as the O(1) space follow-up. The interviewer wants to see if you can explain a complicated concept clearly, not just whether you've memorized the algorithm.

Understanding WHY Floyd's works (cycle entrance = duplicate) is more durable than memorizing the code. 142 is the key problem for internalizing that "why."

### Key insight
For "knowledge-gated" problems like 287, presenting two solutions and explaining the trade-off (space complexity) demonstrates more depth than just producing the optimal answer.

## Theme: Why slow2 from head meets slow at the cycle entrance (Floyd's proof)

Solved 142 (Linked List Cycle II). The key question: after slow and fast meet inside the cycle, why does a second pointer starting from head meet slow exactly at the cycle entrance?

Proof:
- F = distance from head to cycle entrance
- h = distance from entrance to meeting point
- C = cycle length
- slow traveled F + h, fast traveled F + h + C
- Since fast = 2 × slow: F = C - h
- So slow needs F more steps to reach the entrance, and slow2 starting from head also needs F steps — they meet at the entrance.

Corner cases that caused trouble: checking `if not head`, and the while condition `fast and fast.next` (fast.next needed to safely call fast.next.next).

### Key insight
F = C - h is the core equation. Once you derive this, the "start slow2 from head" step is obvious, not magic.
